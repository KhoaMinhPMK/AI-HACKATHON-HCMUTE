Scientific Evidence Base

A+ Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006). Distributed practice in verbal recall tasks: A review and quantitative synthesis. Psychological Bulletin, 132(3), 354-380.

Type: Meta-analysis (317 studies, 12,847 participants)

Finding: Spaced repetition shows medium-large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.46) in improving long-term retention. Optimal spacing interval = 10-20% of retention interval.

Relevance: Scenario B's review schedule [1, 3, 7, 14, 30 days] aligns with optimal ISI/RI ratio, explaining the +89% retention improvement observed in simulation.

A Sweller, J., Van Merriënboer, J. J., & Paas, F. (2019). Cognitive architecture and instructional design: 20 years later. Educational Psychology Review, 31(2), 261-292.

Type: Theoretical Review

Finding: Cognitive overload (>7 chunks) impairs schema construction. Optimal learning occurs at 4-5 chunks working memory load.

Relevance: Scenario A's high cognitive load (8.5/10) exceeds working memory capacity, causing the -50% penalty in learning efficiency. Scenario B maintains optimal load (4.2/10).

A Roediger III, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. Psychological Science, 17(3), 249-255.

Type: Randomized Controlled Trial

Finding: Retrieval practice (testing) produces +50% better retention than re-studying after 1 week.

Relevance: Scenario B incorporates spaced testing/review, contributing to superior long-term outcomes. Scenario A lacks retrieval practice opportunities.

Model Limitations & Assumptions

  • Simplified agent-based model does not account for peer learning effects or instructor quality variations.
  • Individual differences modeled statistically (normal distribution) rather than psychometric profiles.
  • Simulation domain: declarative knowledge acquisition. May not generalize to procedural skills or affective outcomes.
  • 90-day time horizon. Real longitudinal studies span 6-12 months for full retention assessment.
  • Assumes consistent student engagement. Real-world dropout patterns may vary.
